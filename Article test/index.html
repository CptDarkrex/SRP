What is sediment?

Sediment is a naturally occurring material that is broken down by processes of weathering and erosion, and is subsequently transported by the action of wind, water, ice, or by the force of gravity acting down on the particles.

View details »

How does it occur?

Sedimentation is the process of allowing particles in suspension in water or other medium to settle out of suspension under the effect of gravity. The particles that settle out of suspension become sediment and in some cases like water treatment, it is known as sludge.

View more »

What is the self cleansing velocity?

The self cleansing velocity is defined as the minimum velocity at which particles flow at a velocity where there is no deposition or accumulation of particles along the pipe. This is what we will determine in the experiment.

View more »

Aim & Hypothesis

To determine how water velocity affects sedimentation.

If the water velocity drops from the self cleaning velocity the particles will settle out of the suspension creating sedimentation this is because the force of gravity is greater than that of the water.

History
Why is the project important?

Sewers were first developed in the third millennium BCE in the ancient cities of Harappa and Mohenjo-daro. These primitive sewers were carved in the ground alongside buildings, leaving them exposed to the population. They would overflow when storms came and would inflict various diseases to the population

As time progressed sewers evolved, they became underground and started to use gravity to transport waste rather than permitting it to sit still. Ever since, civil engineers have used gravity to transport and collect waste from point “A” to point “B”. The main problem engineers encounter is that gravity only works in one direction, “down”. This constrains the modern sewer system because if the water flow is too fast (due to the angle of the pipe) the walls of the sewer pipe will be damaged over time and if it's too slow the particles in suspension will settle down, resulting in the growth of sediment which over time can fully clog the sewer pipe.

Over the years civil engineers have developed formulas and minimum speeds for sewer pipes to find the balance required to create sewer systems much more efficient and lasting.

Procedure

  1. Mix the water and sand in a bucket
  2. Connect pump and water flow sensor via flexible vinyl tube (12mm)
  3. Connect the main pipe with the water flow sensor using the flexible vinyl tube (12mm)
  4. Place the vinyl pipe horizontal on a table or flat surface with the end aiming at the bucket
  5. Place the pump in the bucket with water and sand
  6. Change the velocity of the water by raising the the pipe in the z-axis
  7. Record the water flow, angle, sedimentation height, and length of pipe.

Discussion

It was expected that the wetted surface would decrease as the water velocity increased and that the particles of sand would remain in suspension after a certain water velocity but would damage the pipe over time if exceeded. The sand would remain in suspension once the water velocity became 0.9 metres per second, this is true because the force acting upon the sand is greater than that of gravity, this force is known as the Brownian motion which is the random motion of particles suspended in a fluid (a liquid or a gas) resulting from their collision with the fast-moving atoms or molecules in the gas or liquid.

Reliability

The reliability of the experiment was limited to the method of which the volume of the sediment was measured. This is because of the percentage of the height of the sediment being estimated rather than measured to the millimetre. Due to this error the results are not to be taken with 100% credibility. To improve the reliability of the experiment one could use trigonometry to work out the height of the sediment which would be accurately measured rather than estimated. The angle at which the pipe stood varied since it was held by hand. This angle could have been systemised if it was placed on a rigging device to lift and hold the pipe at different heights.

Modifications

In light of the experience gained through this experiment, it is clear that smaller angles would have been preferable as they better show the relation between sediment and water velocity more effectively than larger angles. Future experiments with the same aim should contain smaller angles such as 0.1, 1 , 1.5, 2, 2.5 etc.

Implication

There are various implications of this experiment in the workforce. One common example is the process of designing a sewer pipe which uses gravity to transport waste rather than electricity. A small model like the one used in the experiment can be used as a prototype for testing a new project idea.

Conclusion

The aim of the experiment was to determine how water affects sedimentation at varying velocities and it was hypothesised that as the water velocity was increased, the sedimentation would decline due to brownian motion being in play. The findings indicated subtle changes in sedimentation occurring after the water velocity equaled greater than 0.9 metres per second which is the self cleansing velocity. The results of the experiment support the hypothesis that, If the water velocity drops from the self cleaning velocity the particles will settle out of the suspension creating sedimentation.
